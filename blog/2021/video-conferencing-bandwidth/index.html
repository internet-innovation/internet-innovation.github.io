How Much Internet Does Video Conferencing Need?

Created in May 27, 2021

2021   ·   measurement   video-conferencing   covid   ·   data-story

By Kyle MacMillan, Tarun Mangla, James Saxon, and Nick Feamster

Overview

Researchers at the University of Chicago’s Internet Innovation Initiative conducted laboratory experiments to determine bandwidth requirements for popular video conferencing applications during the COVID-19 pandemic. The City of Chicago commissioned this study to help identify households lacking sufficient internet connectivity.

Key Findings

Single Call Performance: The three most-tested applications—Zoom, Google Meet, and Microsoft Teams—required approximately 1-2 Mbps download and 0.75-1.4 Mbps upload speeds for individual calls. Most residential Internet plans provide plenty of capacity to handle this usage, with the FCC’s broadband standard of 25 Mbps download and 3 Mbps upload far exceeding typical needs.

Multiple Simultaneous Calls: Problems emerge when multiple video conferences run simultaneously. The upload limits can come into play with only 2 simultaneous Teams meetings or 3-4 concurrent Zoom or Meet sessions, creating competition for bandwidth. Applications sometimes prioritize certain calls, degrading quality for others.

Meeting Size Impact: Larger meetings with participants using speaker mode increased bandwidth demands, particularly stressing upload capacity on minimum-speed plans.

Service Interruptions: When bandwidth dropped for 30 seconds, apps required 25 to 50 seconds to return to their pre-interruption performance.

Policy Implications

Rather than relying solely on FCC broadband definitions, policymakers should establish household-level benchmarks addressing realistic usage scenarios—such as supporting multiple simultaneous remote school participants or work-from-home situations.

This research is part of the Internet Innovation Initiative at the University of Chicago.
